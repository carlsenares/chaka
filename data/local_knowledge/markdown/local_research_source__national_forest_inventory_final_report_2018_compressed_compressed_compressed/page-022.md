The larger Ethiopian Plateau is also known as the Northwestern Highlands. In the
Southeast there is a smaller plateau designated as the Southeastern Highlands
(Somalian Plateau). Apart from the major river valleys, the whole part of the Somalian
Plateau lies above 1,000 m and about half of the plateau lies above 2,000 m a.s.l.
The Southeastern Plateau is located east and southeast of the Rift system.
The Shewa plateau (2,000 m a.s.l.) occupies the central portion of the Ethiopian Plateau.
It extends west to east with a basalt surface.
The extremely varied topography influences the climate in Ethiopia and its climatic
belts can be defined as follows4:
• Wurch: Frequent frost, usually no rainfed crops would be expected to grow. The
  altitude is above 3,700 m. a.s.l. and the annual rainfall is normally > 900 mm
• High Dega: Average altitude lies between 3,200 and 3,700 m a.s.l., the temperature
  varies between 8 °C and 15 °C and the annual rainfall between 900 and 1,100 mm.
• Dega (cool to cold temperature): Typical of the cool highlandswith average
  temperatures between 10 °C and 16 °C. The altitude is above 2,000 meters above
  the sea level.
• Weina Dega (warm to cool climate): Average annual temperature between 16 °C and 20
  °C. This category contains a big part of the highlands.
• Kolla (warm to hot climate): The climate of the hot lowlands has a temperature
  range in between 20° C to 30° C and altitude ranges from 500 to 1,500 meters above
  the sea level.
• Bereha (Hot and arid climate): Bereha includes the desert lowlands below 500 meters
  a.s.l. and the average annual temperature is over 30° C.
Two distinct seasons characterize the Ethiopia’s climate, the dry season (from October
to May) and rainy season (from June to September) with some relevant variations (3-4
weeks) from east to west.
An upward trend in temperatures (+0.3° C year−1) and a downward trend in rainfall
(0.4 mm month−1 year−1) has been observed since 1948 and is expected to continue up to
20505.
The rainfall pattern is mainly bimodal in the north part of Ethiopia, with a short season
around March or April and a second rainy season that begins in June or July.
The two seasons converge into a unimodal pattern in some areas as in the western and
some northern parts of the country where rainfall is generally higher. In the larger part
of the central highlands, there is a tendency for the two separate seasons6.
