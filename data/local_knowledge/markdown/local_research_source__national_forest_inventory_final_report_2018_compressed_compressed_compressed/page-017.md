Results
Out of the 627 planned sampling units, 539 accessible ones containing a total of 2,077
accessible sample plots were visited. About 49,829 trees and 2,029 stumps were
recorded and analyzed, and a cumulative area of 1,029 ha was surveyed by the field
crews in 27 months.
Approximately 40.3% of the total growing stock in terms of above-ground biomass
(AGB) is located in natural regenerated forests, followed by cultivated land (28.5%)
Other Wooded Land (22.9%), Other Land Natural (5.8%), Planted Forests (1.4%) and
Other Land Built-up (1.1%). Hence, there is a considerable number of trees outside of
forests (ToFs). ToFs are considered as an important part of the total growing stock,
because woodlands dominate the landscape of Ethiopia. Although built-up land does
not have a large share of the national area, it happens to contain a considerable high
number of trees per hectare, which indicates the importance of trees also in the urban
and peri-urban areas. Results by biome demonstrate that in Ethiopia (in both forest
and non- forest land) trees in the Moist Afromontane biome contain 43.3% of the total
volume.
Tree species count shows that the most frequent species in forest are
Crotonmacrostachyus (4.9 trees/ha), Syzygium guineense (4.6 trees/ha) and Juniperus
procera (3.0 trees/ha). In the Other Wooded Land Acacia bussei (5.7 trees/ha) is the
most frequent tree species, followed by Commiphora erythraea (4.8 trees/ha) and
Boswellia rivae (3.7 trees/ha).
Based on the NFI data, 65.8% of forest lands are somehow disturbed, and 58.8% of Other
Wooded Lands are disturbed by human activity. These are rather high figures but typical
to countries, like Ethiopia, with high population pressure on forest resources. In forest
land, the main disturbance is assigned to the encroachment by agriculture. In addition, a
relatively high frequency of Croton macrostachyus, may show that Ethiopia’s forest
ecosystems, particularly in the highlands, are degraded. This species is often an indication
of forest degradation because it colonizes open areas. Further indication of degradation is
the prevalence of Acacia bussei in Other Wooded Land. This species generally tends to
dominate recently regenerated sites in the dry Afromontane areas.
The overall health condition of forests and trees in Ethiopia seems to be at a good level,
approximately 94% of the trees are considered healthy and 3.2% of trees are
unhealthy. The causative agents for unhealthy trees in descending order are: climate,
human, fire, insect pests, deseases and animals.
