All the established sampling units can be considered as permanent and can be revisited
during the next NFI cycles. Remeasurements of the sampling units will allow for better
understanding of the status of forests and especially forest dynamics in the country. A
continuous NFI is recommended and means that a certain proportion of land (or the
sampling units) is re-measured every year (as for example in Finland and Poland). The
continuous NFI method would allow the country to have a non-interrupted flow of up-to-
date information on its forests continuously. Similarly, new variables can be added to the
inventory, if needed. At the moment, Ethiopia is planning to repeat the NFI in 5-year cycles,
but it is recommended that a continuous NFI method be considered as an alternative
option.
All collected data are stored in an Open Foris Collect database and managed by the
MRV Directorate. This database can be used for further analysis and scientific research
purposes. However, it should be noted that the first NFI was designed to give results
primarily for the national level, despite results being reported by region. Therefore, an
intensification of NFI sampling in the future can expand the use of NFI data to the lower
(subnational) levels and improve the accuracy of the estimates.
Furthermore, the NFI methodology can be adapted and improved, by adding new
variables or stratification schemes. In 2016, Ethiopia already institutionalized MRV in a
decentralized way, thus allowing all the administrative levels to be part of the process.
MEFCC has several options to improve NFI mechanisms, through formalization of NFI as
part of MRV regularly carried out by (decentralized) institutions.
For complete and correct adoption of this strategy it is recommended that Ethiopia
improve the following aspects of the NFI:
•   Formal approval of the new national forest definition;
•   Take into account the change in the national forest definition in order to harmonize
    the data analysis and reporting consistently;
•   Improve the stratification system;
•   Improve the forest type classification;
•   Decentralize the field operations so that they are coordinated technically at the
    central level in order to secure faster and more efficient field data collection phase;
•   Establish clear protocols for data flow between the NFI levels (i.e. operators);
•   Reinforce links between remote sensing analysis, inventory planning and field data
    collection;
•   Use NFI field data as validation of RS results (forest cover/change detection analysis);
•   Include forest degradation indicators into the list of attributes to be collected;
•   Include additional temporary sample plots in the inventory;
•   Further training and supervision to reduce the number of measurement errors and
    improve the QA/QC procedures;
•   Assign permanent teams to the NFI in order to create specialized and professional teams;
•   Inclusion of a botanist in each team.
