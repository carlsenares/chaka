Message from the State Minister
                           I am very glad to see that our Ministry, in a few short years of its
                           establishment, managed to complete the first National Forest Inventory
                           (NFI) and produce the NFI Report. I am also pleased to note that this was
                           largely undertaken by Ethiopians with technical support from the Food and
                           Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO), which is yet another
                           big achievement. The national, expert-driven process has helped us build our
                           institutional capacity which will enable periodic forest resources monitoring
                           of the forest cover over time.

As we all know, forests play a vital role in livelihoods of Ethiopians by ensuring food security,
alleviating poverty, providing environmental services (water supply for domestic, agricultural and
energy production), and opportunities for green growth and development, among others. While the
contribution from the forestry sector to gross domestic product (GDP) remains largely
underestimated in official statistics, our recent findings indicate that the sector’s direct contributions
to the national GDP and to other sectors are about 6% and over 7% respectively.

For Ethiopia with fragile mountainous ecosystem, the forests play an even greater role in protecting
the country from erosion, floods, droughts and other adverse impacts of climate change, while
potentially improving rainfall regimes across the country. Clearly, forestry is the most important and
strategic sector that needs due attention by all citizens. Nevertheless, despite such dependence of our
lives on forests, we have lost the resource to deforestation due to population pressure and increasing
demand for agricultural and grazing lands, and unsustainable fuel wood consumption. Cognizant of
this serious threat to the forest resources, the government is addressing deforestation and forest
degradation mainly through its REDD+ program.

Forest resources information is crucial for various strategic decisions, sector policy formulation and
for monitoring forest trends and for this reason, the national forest inventory was designed and
implemented. I am happy that many years after the Woody Biomass Inventory, we have successfully
completed the first NFI which is more comprehensive and holistic, designed to collate a wide range of
information including data for our REDD+ program. The estimates and results from our current NFI
will guide development planning in the sector while making data and information available for
international reporting obligations. The current NFI is not an end; rather it is a beginning for future
periodic monitoring and inventories.

I would like to commend the efforts and hard work of all those involved in the NFI, particularly the
NFI field crew for getting through this challenging work in the field and the coordinating team.

I would also like to thank the Royal Norwegian government for its financial support and the World
Bank and FAO, without the support of which this work would have been impossible. I congratulate all
involved for the successful completion of this tiresome but vital effort.

Finally, I hope that the NFI results will be of help to all stakeholders, besides the Ministry and
government agencies in the sector.

                                             H.E. Kebede Yimam,
                           State Minister of Environment, Forest and Climate Change

Foreword
Understanding forest resources and their changes is key to national and international
environmental and developmental policy processes and information on forest resources is
required by many international agreements, including the United Nations Framework
Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD), the
United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification (UNCCD), the United Nations Forest
Instrument (UNFI) and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

Consequently, the demand for reliable and up-to-date forest information at national level has
grown considerably. The 16th and 19th Conference of the Parties (COP 16 and COP 19)
decided that robust national forest monitoring systems (NFMS) should provide data and
information that are transparent and consistent over time, which are suitable for measuring,
reporting and verifying (MRV) anthropogenic forest-related emissions by sources and
removals by sinks, forest carbon stocks, and forest-area changes for REDD+.

Ethiopia’s national forest inventory (NFI), the first of its type, involved a detailed field survey
of the country’s forests to assess the extent, composition, biomass and condition of the entire
national forest estate by visiting 539 Sampling Units (2,077 permanent forest inventory
plots) and spanned from February 2014 to July 2016. These plots now represent a bank of
baseline data. NFI analysis results were computed using general models, formulas and
conversion factors and are presented by four biomes. The result of this survey will enable
Ethiopia to fulfill national and international reporting obligations and to monitor the
sustainable development of the forest resource in Ethiopia. Data from the NFI will be used to
estimate carbon stocks through the calculation of forest biomass in forests and greenhouse
gas emissions and reductions associated with land use change.

Data processing and analysis was completed to allow for the preparation of this publication in
April 2018, the preparation of which was a highly collaborative process involving MEFCC and
FAO. The NFI results were subjected to a thorough assessment including a validation
workshop before publication. This important publication is the result of hard work of all who
were engaged in the consultation process. As you read the report, I hope you will gain insight
into the breadth and depth of our activities.

This NFI report is structured in five Sections. Section 1 provides general background on
Ethiopia’s commitment to carbon-neutral development and its relevant strategy that leads to
achieving its vision and the NFI is one of the main elements that enables decision making.
Section 2 deals with methodology for undertaking the NFI and data analysis followed by
results in Section 3. Conclusion and the way forward are given in Section 4. The contents of
the different sections are summarized in the Executive Summary Section while details are
provided in the appendices.

Executive Summary
In the first quarter of 2016, the Government of Ethiopia (GoE) announced that the
number of people affected by severe drought caused by El Niño stood at 10.2 million in
the period 2015/2016.
Ethiopia is seriously committed to addressing climate change. The Climate Resilient
Green Economy (CRGE) strategy that was officially introduced during the 17th
Conference of the Parties (COP 17) in Durban in 2011 is a clear manifestation of
commitment to pursue a carbon-neutral development regime. The CRGE strategy has
identified a number of low carbon-emitting initiatives across key economic sectors
(forestry, agriculture, energy, livestock, transport and industry) that will help Ethiopia
achieve its vision of building a climate-resilient middle-income economy by 2025.The
successful implementation of these initiatives would help Ethiopia eliminate projected
emissions of over 250 Mt CO2e a year by 2030 compared to the actual Business as
Usual (BAU) scenario. The strategy aims to maintain the annual Greenhouse Gas (GHG)
level of the country at the 2010’s level, i.e., 150 Mt CO2e.
The forests in the country are under direct and indirect threats. Direct threats include
small and large-scale agricultural land conversion, increased wood extraction for fuel
and construction, and pressure caused by increased livestock grazing. The indirect
threats comprise gaps in the application of forest policy and regulations; tenure/unclear
forest user rights; lack of private investment in forestry development; population
growth; and inadequate land use planning and participatory forest management (PFM).
As a result, the forestry sector is the second largest contributor of GHG emissions in the
country after agriculture. Under BAU, greater pressure on forests will increase the rate
of deforestation and forest degradation and prevent the country from reaping its long-
term environmental and economic benefits.
In this context the knowledge of the status of the forest and the Trees Outside of Forest
(TOF) is a country priority. The CRGE strategy includes the adoption of the REDD+
programme as operational instrument for mitigate the CO2 emissions in the forest sector.


              Objectives
Ethiopia conducted the National Forest Inventory (NFI) field campaign between 2014
and 2016. The NFI started as a FAO - Technical Co-operation Project (TCP) in 2013 with
the aim to pilot and test the National Forest Inventory methodology, and was then
scaled up with the aid of Forest Carbon Partnership Facility (FCPF) and Bio-Carbon
Fund for broader implementation of Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and
Forest Degradation (REDD+) Readiness at the Ministry of Environment, Forest and
Climate Change (MEFCC) in late 2014 and continued with the FAO technical support.

Specific objectives of the Ethiopian NFI were:
     •   The identification of a methodological approach to support the Measuring,
         Reporting and Verification (MRV) implementation by taking into account the
         national context through a participatory process;
     • The design and set up of for a long-term monitoring system of the forests and trees
       outside of forest based on a network of permanent plots located across the country;
     • The collection of variables that respond to the national data needs;
     • The establishment of a database to store and manage the field information for
       analysis and dissemination.
     The forest definition adopted for NFI is: Forest as a land with of 10% tree canopy cover, a
     0.5 ha area and a 5 m tree height in average.


                      Methodology
     The sampling design chosen for NFI is stratified systematic cluster sampling. Using
     available geospatial layers of Ethiopia and large scale ecological studies the whole
     country was classified into five strata that give a macro ecological characterization of
     five macro areas. The number of the sampling unitsvarieswithin the strata depending on
     the estimated probability to find trees and forests. A total of 627 sampling units were
     established across the five strata. Every sampling unit has an area of 1 km2 and is
     composed of 4 plots (with cumulative plot area of 2 ha).
     A series of variables were chosen to give a complete characterization of Ethiopian
     forests, including biomass, carbon, biodiversity, health status and disturbances. The
     quality of the final results is ensured by rigorous Quality Assessment/Quality Control
     (QA/QC) procedures.
     The methodology adopted can be replicated as all the sampling units are permanent
     and the data collection can be improved according to the findings on this first field
     campaign. All the collected NFI data has been stored in a database at the MRV
     laboratory of the MEFCC.
     In 2015 the stratification scheme was changed because Ethiopia decided to adopt a
     classification that better describes the vegetation characteristics of the country. The
     following biomes were adopted:

               Acacia-Commiphora
               Combretum-Terminalia
               Dry Afromontane
               Moist Afromontane
     This change resulted in the adoption of more specific analysis methods. All the results
     are thus presented by biome, and not by original NFI strata. All results have been
     computed using general models, formulas and conversion factors described in this
     report.

Results
Out of the 627 planned sampling units, 539 accessible ones containing a total of 2,077
accessible sample plots were visited. About 49,829 trees and 2,029 stumps were
recorded and analyzed, and a cumulative area of 1,029 ha was surveyed by the field
crews in 27 months.
Approximately 40.3% of the total growing stock in terms of above-ground biomass
(AGB) is located in natural regenerated forests, followed by cultivated land (28.5%)
Other Wooded Land (22.9%), Other Land Natural (5.8%), Planted Forests (1.4%) and
Other Land Built-up (1.1%). Hence, there is a considerable number of trees outside of
forests (ToFs). ToFs are considered as an important part of the total growing stock,
because woodlands dominate the landscape of Ethiopia. Although built-up land does
not have a large share of the national area, it happens to contain a considerable high
number of trees per hectare, which indicates the importance of trees also in the urban
and peri-urban areas. Results by biome demonstrate that in Ethiopia (in both forest
and non- forest land) trees in the Moist Afromontane biome contain 43.3% of the total
volume.
Tree species count shows that the most frequent species in forest are
Crotonmacrostachyus (4.9 trees/ha), Syzygium guineense (4.6 trees/ha) and Juniperus
procera (3.0 trees/ha). In the Other Wooded Land Acacia bussei (5.7 trees/ha) is the
most frequent tree species, followed by Commiphora erythraea (4.8 trees/ha) and
Boswellia rivae (3.7 trees/ha).
Based on the NFI data, 65.8% of forest lands are somehow disturbed, and 58.8% of Other
Wooded Lands are disturbed by human activity. These are rather high figures but typical
to countries, like Ethiopia, with high population pressure on forest resources. In forest
land, the main disturbance is assigned to the encroachment by agriculture. In addition, a
relatively high frequency of Croton macrostachyus, may show that Ethiopia’s forest
ecosystems, particularly in the highlands, are degraded. This species is often an indication
of forest degradation because it colonizes open areas. Further indication of degradation is
the prevalence of Acacia bussei in Other Wooded Land. This species generally tends to

1.4            Forest definitions
In order to correctly frame the objectives of this report it is important to review the
definitions of forest adopted in Ethiopia during the period 1994-201517.
According to the Federal negarit gazeta, ”forest” means a community of plants either
naturally grown or developed by planting and mainly consisting of trees and other
plants having woody character18. This definition was later changed to better describe
the Ethiopian forests for reporting purposes and included in the FRA reporting19. This
definition describes the forest as a land with a tree canopy cover ≥ 10%, a 0.5 ha area
and a 5m tree height in average.
This definition was adopted to define the forest in the NFI methodology and it is also
the definition adopted for this report.
However, Ethiopia adopted a new forest definition20 in February 2015: Land spanning
at least 0.5 ha covered by trees (including bamboo) (with a minimum width of 20 m or
not more than two-thirds of its length) attaining a height of at least 2 m and a canopy
cover of at least 20% or trees with the potential to reach these thresholds in situ in due
course.
Currently, Ethiopia is in the process of approving this new forest definition as its
national legal definition of forest. The reason for changing the national forest definition
is to better capture the natural primary state of forest vegetation. Specifically, the reason
to reduce the tree height criteria from 5m to 2m is to capture natural forest vegetation
types like the dryland forests which hosts woody species that typically reach a height of
around 2-3 m.
The proposed change results in the inclusion areas which previously were classified as
Ethiopia’s dense woodlands into forest. Because commercial agriculture is expanding
mainly on dense woodlands, Ethiopia wishes to enable REDD+ incentives for its
conservation of this land type. The reason for increasing the canopy cover threshold
from 10% to 20% is to avoid inclusion of highly degraded forest lands into the forest
land and in this way to provide incentives for protecting quality forest. This definition
of forest is the one adopted for the Forest Reference Emission Level (FREL)21.
The new forest definition differs from the one used for reporting GHG emissions and
removals from the forestry sector within the framework of the Clean Development
Mechanism (CDM)22. This is a minimum of 0.05 ha of land covered by trees attaining a
height of more than 2 m and a canopy cover of more than 20%.

2.10              Wood densities
Wood density data of over 400 tree species found in Ethiopia has been
analyzed. The wood density data was compiled in order to support precise
wood biomass and carbon stock assessment; hence to assist the derivation
of accurate emission factors for the FREL reporting. Wood density is the
expression of the ratio of solid material to air/water space in the wood. It
becomes an important parameter for improving model-based estimation of
biomass and carbon content of woody plants. In forest inventories, wood
density is usually expressed as t·m−3.
Most wood density reports in the scientific literature are reporting basic
density. However, a large number of scientific papers also report air dry
density, and options exist to convert between air dry density and basic
density. One such option is multiplying the basic density by a factor of 1.22
to obtain air-dry density or multiplying air-dry density by a factor of 0.82 to
obtain basic density. Wood density is affected by both tree and
environmental factors such as age, sampling position (radially and
longitudinally within the tree), and edaphic and climatic factors that affect
growth rate. Therefore, results from individual samples, depending on site
of sampling, position of sample from the stem and age of the sampled tree
may yield different results for a single tree. This is the reason why different
values are often observed for the same species in different documents. For
the NFI analysis, the ones with the highest quality have been selected and
applied. In order to assign a reliable value for each of the tree species
inventoried in Ethiopia and missing in the country databases, the Global
Wood Density Database (GWDDB)42 has been adopted by using an R script
through the RStudio environment and the BIOMASS43 package. After the
operation from the National Data Base, only the values identified as Low
Quality Data were excluded and replaced by the GWDDB data. As the result
is out of 360 species identified during the NFI cycle, wood densities of 341
species have been selected using a validated value.

4              Conclusions and way
               forward
The Ethiopian NFI was designed by following the FAO-NFMA methodology with
technical support provided by FAO. The objective of the NFI is to obtain countrywide
baseline information for the forest sector. The NFI was further enriched when the
country decided to participate in the REDD+ mechanism. NFI is an essential part of the
national forest monitoring and MRV systems for REDD+. Conducting the NFI helped
Ethiopia to increase its capacity and gain valuable experiences in implementing MRV of
forest resources through extensive capacity building and awareness raising of topics
such as reference/baseline scenario construction, NFI design and implementation,
monitoring of forest area change, statistics, data processing and reporting. More than
150 Ethiopian technicians have received training in data collection, analysis and
reporting methods at the federal and regional levels during the project.
The NFI and MRV systems will allow the Government to assess its own progress in
reducing emissions from deforestation and degradation and in increasing forest carbon
stocks. In addition, a strengthened forest information database and improved capacity
will place Ethiopia in a better position for formulation of policy for sustainable forest
management, implementing best practices in forest management and establishing a
complete national forest assessment monitoring system. Thus, the NFI provides
baseline information that can support Ethiopia to improve management of its lands
and particularly forest lands, to improve the strategic planning of the fuel wood
production and to further improve the national and subnational forest information
systems. Since the NFI collected data in all land types, including also areas outside of
forests, the NFI results will give a preliminary but exhaustive overview on the status of
trees outside of forests at the national and subnational levels too.
The NFI was designed to provide information for REDD+, and therefore new methods,
tools and collected data stemming from this initiative have improved the knowledge on
current forest carbon stock, emission factor(s) and other relevant forest information on
forest use and forest cover. The information derived from the NFI was used to calculate
and report the emission factors for the first Ethiopian forest reference level submission in
2016.
In 27 months of field data collection, a total of 49,400 trees were recorded in 1,029 plots in
539 sampling units in all the regions of the country. The results show that the population
pressure is high, because 65.8% of forest lands are somehow disturbed and 58.8% of
OWLs are disturbed by human activity. In forest land the main cause of forest
disturbance is encroachment by agriculture. Results on the basal area in forest lands also
indicate that forests appear to be degraded, with BA in forests13.3 m2ha-1 and in High
woodland only 3.8 m2ha-1. The basal area results by biome (forest only plots), in Acacia-
Commiphora, Combretum-Terminalia, Dry Afromontane and Moist Afromontane
estimates are 6.5, 8.4, 12.4 and 20.7 m2ha-1, respectively. Only Moist Afromontane
forest has a basal area greater than 20 m2ha-1.

             69                 Conclusions and way forward


## Page 90

All the established sampling units can be considered as permanent and can be revisited
during the next NFI cycles. Remeasurements of the sampling units will allow for better
understanding of the status of forests and especially forest dynamics in the country. A
continuous NFI is recommended and means that a certain proportion of land (or the
sampling units) is re-measured every year (as for example in Finland and Poland). The
continuous NFI method would allow the country to have a non-interrupted flow of up-to-
date information on its forests continuously. Similarly, new variables can be added to the
inventory, if needed. At the moment, Ethiopia is planning to repeat the NFI in 5-year cycles,
but it is recommended that a continuous NFI method be considered as an alternative
option.
All collected data are stored in an Open Foris Collect database and managed by the
MRV Directorate. This database can be used for further analysis and scientific research
purposes. However, it should be noted that the first NFI was designed to give results
primarily for the national level, despite results being reported by region. Therefore, an
intensification of NFI sampling in the future can expand the use of NFI data to the lower
(subnational) levels and improve the accuracy of the estimates.
Furthermore, the NFI methodology can be adapted and improved, by adding new
variables or stratification schemes. In 2016, Ethiopia already institutionalized MRV in a
decentralized way, thus allowing all the administrative levels to be part of the process.
MEFCC has several options to improve NFI mechanisms, through formalization of NFI as
part of MRV regularly carried out by (decentralized) institutions.
For complete and correct adoption of this strategy it is recommended that Ethiopia
improve the following aspects of the NFI:
•   Formal approval of the new national forest definition;
•   Take into account the change in the national forest definition in order to harmonize
    the data analysis and reporting consistently;
•   Improve the stratification system;
•   Improve the forest type classification;
•   Decentralize the field operations so that they are coordinated technically at the
    central level in order to secure faster and more efficient field data collection phase;
•   Establish clear protocols for data flow between the NFI levels (i.e. operators);
•   Reinforce links between remote sensing analysis, inventory planning and field data
    collection;
•   Use NFI field data as validation of RS results (forest cover/change detection analysis);
•   Include forest degradation indicators into the list of attributes to be collected;
•   Include additional temporary sample plots in the inventory;
•   Further training and supervision to reduce the number of measurement errors and
    improve the QA/QC procedures;
•   Assign permanent teams to the NFI in order to create specialized and professional teams;
•   Inclusion of a botanist in each team.

Finally, it is strongly recommended to reinforce links with the academia by allowing
the research teams to access the NFI data and to improve the quality of the variables
collected. This will yield an increased number of topical publications, thesis, and books

Appendix A10                  Agro-ecological zones
In 2000 the Ministry of Agriculture (MOA) developed the Agro-Ecological Zone (AEZ)
map with the aim to introduce a classification comprehensive of all the relevant
characters and not discipline oriented. The AEZ classification1 is based on the basic
ecological elements of climate, physiography, soils, vegetation, farming systems, etc.
The major characters included in the AEZs are eighteen, delineated and named by
terms describing the broad moisture and elevation conditions of areas. The final AEZ
map classifies Ethiopia into 33 agro-ecological sub-zones identified as homogeneous in
terms of climate, physiography, soils, vegetation, land use, farming system and animal
production. The AEZ has been combined with the Digital Elevation Model (DEM) to
develop the Strata (4.2.3 Argo- ecological zones) used as pre-stratification layer for the
NFA sampling.

Appendix A11                   Potential vegetation map
Published in 2010 the Atlas of potential Vegetation of Ethiopia1, a map in scale
1:2000.000, aims to classify the Ethiopian flora in twelve potential vegetation types
and subtypes.
1. Desert and semi-desert scrubland.
2. Acacia-Commiphora woodland and bushland (with the subtypes (2a) Acacia-
   Commiphora woodland and bushland proper and (2b) Acacia wooded grassland of
   the Rift Valley).
3. Wooded grassland of the western Gambela region.
4. Combretum-Terminalia woodland and wooded grassland.
5. Dry evergreen Afromontane forest and grassland complex (with the subtypes (5a)
   Undifferentiated Afromontane forest, (5b) Dry single-dominant Afromontane forest
   of the Ethiopian highlands, (5c) Afromontane woodland, wooded grassland and
   grassland, (5d) Transition between Afromontane vegetation and Acacia-
   Commiphora bushland on the Eastern escarpment).
6. Moist evergreen Afromontane forest (with the subtypes (6a) Primary or mature
   secondary moist evergreen Afromontane forest, and (6b) Edges of moist evergreen
   Afromontane forest, bushland, woodland and wooded grassland.
7. Transitional rainforest.
8. Ericaceous belt.
9. Afroalpine belt.
10. Riverine vegetation.
11. Fresh-water lakes, etc. (with the subtypes (na) Fresh-water lake vegetation (open
    water) and (nb) Freshwater marshes and swamps, floodplains and lake shore
    vegetation).
12. Salt lakes, etc. (with the subtypes (12a) Salt lake vegetation (open-water) and (12b)
    Saltpans, saline/brackish and intermittent wetlands and salt-lake shore vegetation).
The Atlas has been realized using SRTM DEM of Ethiopia, field surveys, rainfall
datasets, the Global Lakes and Wetlands Database (GLWD) data and by revising the Flora
of Ethiopia and Eritrea 2.The Atlas of Potential Vegetation in Ethiopia as been used in the
definitions of the Biomes (4.2.3.Potential vegetation of Ethiopia).

This report describes the creation of a wall-to-wall biomass map done for Ethiopia
within the National Forest Inventory project. The map is referenced for the year 2016.
The main idea is to use the field inventory data and use it for the calibration of a multi-
sensor satellite data stack, consisting of optical and radar imagery, in order to get
spatially explicit values of biomass. While those estimates are affected by high
uncertainties on a pixel level, and therefore are critical to use for carbon stock
estimates, the map can give important information on the locations where the actual
biomass deviates considerably from the expected one. The spatial information can then
be used for planning of carbon stock enhancements.

Data preparation

NFI data

For the combination of the field and satellite data, the spatial scale and potential
location errors of both datasets has to be taken into account. The sampling unit design
consists of 4 subplots with a respective size of about 2 ha. In order to account for
location errors of the satellite data and the field inventory plots, the 4 subplots have
been aggregated by considering their mean biomass value per Sampling Unit (SU). The
location of the whole SU has therefore been generalized by calculating the center point
of the available subplots. Subsequently, a buffer zone of 300 m radius is created around
this point and the mean value of the subplot biomass measurements is attributed to
that area.

Optical data

A cloud-free wall-to-wall mosaic of optical Landsat-8 data has been generated by using
the System for Earth Observation Data Access, Processing and Analysis for Land
Monitoring (SEPAL) main interface. This allows to create a best-pixel composite
algorithm, that according to some basic parameter settings, detects a set of scenes for a
given year and area. One of the most important parameters is the target day. This
datum decides on the importance of the selected scenes and will give precedence to
the scenes that are closest

to that day. For the given task the 1st of November 2016 has been chosen. This day falls
within the beginning of the dry period and should ideally pick up the vegetation signal
of still photosynthetically active woody vegetation, while the herbaceous layer already
dried out. In this way the reflectance is most related to aboveground woody biomass.
The minimum number of scenes per path/row combination had been set to 10,
assuring cloud-free observations for every pixel. The weighting factor between cloud-
freeness and target day importance was kept on 50%. Apart from the shadow
tolerance, which was set to the maximum, all other parameter settings have been kept
to the default values. Snow masking, BRDF correction as well as the median composite
option were activated for the final retrieval of the mosaic. The reason for activating the
median option were mainly agricultural fields that dependent on the pixel selection
resulted in non-homogeneous artifacts in the final mosaic.
The final mosaic was subsequently downloaded for all of Ethiopia, including all
available bands (i.e. blue, green, red, NIR, SWIR 1 and SWIR 2) on a geometric
resolution of 30 m. Further band ratios have been calculated, i.e. the NDVI, MSAVI,
NDWI and the NBR.

ALOS Kyoto & Carbon mosaic

Long-wavelength L-band data (wavelength = 23 cm) demonstrated its sensitivity to
structural forest attributes such as biomass. While there is no free and open access to
raw data, the Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) provides already
preprocessed data tiles for ALOS PALSAR data. These datasets are generated on a yearly
basis and exhibit a spatial resolution of 25 m. The Open SAR Toolkit (OST) on the
SEPAL platform eases access, applies a Refined Lee Speckle filter and adds a ratio band
for RGB visualization. The 2016 mosaic, based on data from ALOS-2 PALSAR-2, was
used for the elaboration of the biomass map.

Sentinel-1 timescan mosaic

C-band radar data (wavelength = 5 cm) is actually less suited for biomass mapping
itself since the backscatter is rather a function of the canopy structure than the
structure of the tree. In addition, the signal is more sensitive to changes in soil moisture
and the roughness of the surface. However, the dense temporal sampling of the
Sentinel-1 data allows to aggregate the temporal dynamics by calculating basic
descriptive statistics, such as mean, minimum, maximum and standard deviation, in
time. This is referred to as timescan method and enhances the signal with respect to
single imagery. For the mapping, 7 wall- to-wall mosaics between September 2016 and
May 2017 and a spatial resolution of 30 m, were created. This included about 310 full
Sentinel-1 scenes. The resultant time-series data of the full country has then been used
to calculate the respective timescan layers for both the VV and the VH polarization
bands, respectively. All processing has been done with OST on the SEPAL platform,
which provides a fully-automated workflow for the data download, processing and
mosaicking.

SRTM

The Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM) provides elevation data for most parts
of the earth. Recent studies on biomass mapping integrate this data into machine
learning algorithms for better estimates. For the biomass map of Ethiopia, the height
and slope information were derived on a 90 m spatial resolution.

Multi-Sensor Integration

All the above-mentioned satellite data has been merged to a single image stack
consisting of 24 layers. A visual inspection did not result in any data offsets, so that a
separate co- registration of the different layers was not necessary. The extent and pixel
spacing of the Landsat data was used as a reference for the other datasets. Therefore,
all layers were resampled to a 30 m resolution.

Classification

For the integration of the NFI data with the satellite data, a data-driven approach based
on machine learning was chosen. This approach is integrated into the OST and was
consequently run on SEPAL. In detail, the random forest regression algorithm (RF) was
used to create a model between the averaged biomass values for each of the
generalized SUs, and the average value of each of the satellite layers underlying the
generalized SUs. While this includes spatial averaging, it is assumed to account for
localization errors and spatial inhomogeneity. RF showed to be highly efficient for non-
linear relations between a set of multiple predictors (i.e. the satellite data) and the
target parameter (i.e. biomass), while at the same time easy to parameterize.
During the model elaboration, different parameter sets for the classifier are tested on
the fly. The best model was chosen based on the Out-Of-Bag estimate from the RF
classifier itself, and a Leave 5-out cross-validation procedure. In addition, the
importance of features has been used to create a subset of input features (i.e. the
satellite layers) for an additional parameter testing. All those steps are applied to avoid
overfitting of the final model, which RF is prone to when using correlated feature input.
Once the best model has been selected, it is applied on the unseen satellite data in
order to derive the final biomass estimates. While the model has been elaborated on a
reduced resolution of the satellite data (underlying the 300 m radius SU), it was applied
on the full resolution of 30 m, which represents the geometric resolution of the final
product.
In order to attribute for the structural differences of forests between the different
biome zones of Ethiopia, the country has been split into 4 zones beforehand (Figure 1).
The above- mentioned model elaboration was then applied for each zone separately.
This assures that the model represents the relation between the satellite data and the
biomass data according to the specific forest biome, which is assumed to be
homogeneous. One issue is that within the ALOS and Sentinel-1 data missing values
appeared due to strong topography. Due to the side-looking geometry of SAR
instruments, in some cases no information can be retrieved at steep slopes. In these
cases, the same classification approach has been followed for those
