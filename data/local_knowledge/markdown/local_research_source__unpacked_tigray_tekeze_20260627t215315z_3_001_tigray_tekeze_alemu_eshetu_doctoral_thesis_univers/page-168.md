Over 90% of households throughout the Waghimra Zone live on smallholder farming with
landholdings of less than one hectare. The agricultural activities are rain-fed, with the main
harvest from September to November. Farmers produce mainly cereals (sorghum, teff, barley,
and wheat), pulses, and vegetables. Livestock, like cattle, donkey, sheep and goat, chicken and
bees, are kept for the consumption of their products, plowing, sale, and as savings mechanism
to cope with economic stress.
The region has severe land degradation, climate variability, and the highest population density
(Rosell and Holmer 2007; Hermans-Neumann et al. 2017). The zone has been one of the most
drought-affected areas in the past. High vulnerability to drought and famine, growing
population pressure, and land degradation have reduced yield per capita. People residing in the
study area are frequently in immediate need of food/relief assistance. Migration for short-term
employment has been a feature of the livelihoods of the poorest in Waghimra. Due to the
consequences of climate change, and low and erratic rainfall patterns, the food system's
capacity to provide decent livelihoods is critically restrained in the upper Tekeze basin.
