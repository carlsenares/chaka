7.3.6. Government institutions and political factors
7.3.6.1. Land tenure

Customary land rights were established in Konso during the time of the influx of various
clans and the occupation of the current land territory (Beshah, 2003). Watson (2009)
pointed out that the first land ownership is established by clearing or burning previously
uncultivated land, while ownership of land after that is acquired by inheritance and
purchase. Although there were various Ethiopian regimes’ land policies, the land in
Konso is both private and state-owned. Private land “ownership” means the right to use,
sell, lend, or pass on to heirs (Watson, 2009). Theoretically, the imperial regime before
1941, the Derg regime, and the current government declared the land as state and public
property, but practically, it is not applicable in Konso. The land is often sold informally,
while the formal court does not recognize it. The buyers of the land are even remembered
after their deaths via the statue (Waakka) and stone poles erected near their tombs. The
number of erected stone poles near the grave indicates the number of farm plots the
deceased person bought while he was alive. This suggests that private ownership of land
in Konso society was institutionalized from the beginning and has been passed down
from generation to generation.
