X      Uniform scale of data collection: Thematic maps in a watershed should have the same scale to
       facilitate the transfer of information or the production of subsequent maps by superimposing one
       on another (conducting overlay analysis). This can be achieved by preparing uniform data collection
       units (25 m × 25 m) for many attributes with the aid of GIS and GPS tools. This offers the possibility
       to analyze parameters spatially and compare the different parameters under various prevailing
       management conditions.
X      Hierarchy of data collection: To understand the relationship of different processes that are affected
       by the spatial aspects of soil, topographic, geologic, geomorphologic, hydrologic, land use and
       land cover, land management, and farming system elements and to achieve data transferability,
       we could use a different hierarchy with different spatial scales (point, micro-plot, plot, household,
       community, sub-watershed, and/or watershed). The research concept involves, among other things,
       several field- and watershed-level research experiments for comparative studies. For example, the
       functionality and degree of performance of interventions on gullies, water harvesting/water points,
       and other infrastructure can be surveyed and assessed at feature scales. Soil erosion processes
       and crop productivity data should be measured at micro-plot, plot, and field levels. To determine the
       spatial variability of the impact of land management practices on sediment yield and groundwater
       recharge, small stream flows and soil accumulation capacity of structures can be evaluated at sub-
       catchment scale. Climate, land use/land cover, land management distribution, geomorphology,
       geology, hydrogeology, discharge, erosion damage (rill and gully), and sediment yield are documented
       at watershed scale. Socioeconomic parameters (perceptions, adoption, and cost-benefit analysis)
       are measured at household and community scale.
