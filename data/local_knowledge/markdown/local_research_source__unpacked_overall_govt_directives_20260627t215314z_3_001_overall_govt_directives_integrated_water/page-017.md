2. Purpose of the Guideline
Integrated watershed management is an approach to nurture the integrated use and management of land,
vegetation, water, and other watershed resources to benefit people, protect the watershed ecosystem,
and reduce or avoid negative downstream impacts (German et al., 2007). It is meant to manage human
activities and natural resources together in a watershed as a system. Changes in environmental costs and
socioeconomic impacts result from natural soil erosion, changes in farming systems, over-abstraction of
water, overgrazing, deforestation, and pollution. Watershed management encompasses interventions that
meet diverse stakeholder and ecosystem needs under various land uses and farming systems: highland
agriculture farming system, lowland agriculture farming system, forest system, agro-pastoralist and
pastoralist system, and upstream and downstream communities. Depending on the prevailing conditions,
watershed management could have one or more objectives: agricultural production and food security,
water management and security, drought management, flood management, conservation of biodiversity,
and so on. Nevertheless, because of the strong interconnection among watershed resources, watershed
management interventions focusing on a single component of the watershed or single objective might
result in multiple and far-reaching impacts.
We recognize that watersheds constitute both human and natural systems. However, this guideline
focuses mainly on natural systems and processes (soil, water, vegetation, climate, and topography
interaction) and is limited to current socioeconomic and livelihood-related interventions. This guideline
adopts a micro-watershed scale of a minimum size of 500 ha, which is aligned with the national
community-based integrated watershed management guideline.
We used the following assumptions in preparing this guideline:
X   Many watersheds do not constitute a full-fledged data monitoring facility and have field experiments
    to employ advanced data collection methods.
X   Selected methods and procedures presented in this guideline are identified based on the technical
    capability of most experts and average watershed conditions.
X   The type of data requirements in a watershed are considered based on systems thinking.
X   Interaction of local knowledge and empirical testing methods is taken into consideration.
X   Future updates are necessary to reflect advanced methods and tools for watershed monitoring and
    evaluation (such as automatic data loggers, radar, photogrammetry, remote sensing, etc.) as well
    as the dynamics in watershed management. Regular updates ensure the workability of the data
    collection standards in relation to inclusiveness and utility by most experts and researchers.
