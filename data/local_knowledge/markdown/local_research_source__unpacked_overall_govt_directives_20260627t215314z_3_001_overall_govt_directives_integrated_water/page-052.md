5.3.14 Reservoir sediment yield survey
Artificial water reservoirs are constructed for irrigation development, drinking, aquaculture, hydropower,
flood control, or combinations. However, the presence of reservoirs (natural or man-made) creates an
excellent impoundment to trap sediment during transportation. Successive reservoir surveys provide
information on the rate of soil erosion and sediment yield from the catchment of the reservoir. Standard
methods exist for conducting reservoir surveys for the estimation of soil erosion rate from the catchment.


Overlay analysis of successive maps
For man-made reservoirs, it is necessary to acquire a topographic map of the reservoirs during the
design phase and develop a Digital Elevation Model (DEM). Water depth could be measured using different
methods, for example: (a) sonic depth recorders, (b) bathymetric surveys, and (c) grid-square method in
dry reservoirs. Based on these data, a topographic map of the current topography of the reservoir could
be constructed at the same scale as the original map. Digitize the same maps and develop a data layer
between the two DEMs. The volume difference between the two maps indicates the sediment stored over
the years, that is, between survey intervals, which can be calculated using GIS software. This method can
be applied when the quantity of inflow and outflow from reservoirs is known in each measurement period.
The following options can be used:
a.   Sonic depth recorders
Sonic depth recorders are used to measure water depth along predetermined ranges and locations.
Sediment yield can be calculated from previous surveys of the original reservoir bottom or from the
soundings of the sediment depth.
b.   Area-elevation-capacity curve
The area and capacity of the reservoir are determined from the topographic map of the reservoir mapped
