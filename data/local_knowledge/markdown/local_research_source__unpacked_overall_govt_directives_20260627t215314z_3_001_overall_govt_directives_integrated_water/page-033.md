Wind speed
Wind speed can be recorded using an anemometer, usually at 2 m above ground level (Figure 2). Several
different units are used to indicate wind speed, such as meters per second (m/s), kilometers per hour
(km/h), miles per hour (mph), feet per second (ft/s), and knots (kt). Later, data should be converted to
standard SI units (preferably, km/h).

Figure 2. Anemometer for measuring wind speed and wind vane for measuring wind direction.




Source: Desjardins et al., 1986.


Wind direction
Wind direction is described in terms of the direction from which it blows. Wind direction can be recorded
using a wind vane at least in terms of north, northeast, east, southeast, south, southwest, west, and
northwest. If required, wind direction could be represented by one of the 16 directions.


Relative humidity
Relative humidity is the ratio of the current absolute humidity to the highest possible absolute humidity.
A device to measure relative humidity is called a hygrometer. When water or ice covers the bulb of a
thermometer (wet-bulb), latent heat is removed from the surface of the bulb as the water evaporates, and
the wet-bulb temperature becomes lower than the air (dry-bulb) temperature. At a lower humidity, water
evaporates more actively, so that the wet-bulb temperature lowers sharply. An aspirated psychrometer
(Figure 3) measures humidity by measuring the difference between the dry-bulb temperature and wet-
bulb temperature. Vapor and saturated vapor pressure tables (provided together with the instrument)
should be used to calculate the relative humidity from the bulb reading (Table 9).
