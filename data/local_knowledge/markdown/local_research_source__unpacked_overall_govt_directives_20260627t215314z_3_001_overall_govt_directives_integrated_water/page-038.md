Methods: To develop large-scale land use/cover information, Landsat images are not appropriate for
highly fragmented and heterogeneous terrains. Rather, high-resolution Google Earth images that are
available online can be used. Ortho-photos, which are under development by the Ministry of Agriculture
with a resolution of 40 cm, can also be used. Using Google Earth images, detailed homogeneous mapping
units (MUs) for each micro-watershed can be developed. Each MU is defined based on the homogeneity
of the landscape’s basic characteristics such as land use/cover, terrain condition (altitude, slope angle,
slope shape, aspect), soil erosion features, and landforms. Considering the possibility of mappable
features (spatial configuration of land features) in the watershed and the required level of detail of
information, the convenient smallest size of MUs can be defined to be 25 m × 25 m (625 m2, or approximate
data collection scale of 1:2,500 to 1:10,000). This allows us to generate the maximum possible detailed
information such as settlements, small plantation/woodlots, and patches of forest around churches.
The first task after developing the MU is labeling each MU with detailed LULC classes (Table 14). This
can be done in the office by employing object-oriented screen digitization on a higher-resolution Google
Earth image. The digitized MUs are produced on A2 or A3 paper or uploaded to a Notepad for the field
work. During the field work, remapping of the MUs is done using GPS by taking ground truth. Whenever
the boundary of the mapping units does not match, remerging and/or splitting is done. In each MU, the
major predefined land use/cover classes and their extent are identified and collected based on expert
and local people’s judgment through a transect walk. To detect change in land use/cover, this method
can be used to reconstruct land use/cover of previous periods by consulting key informants and focus
group discussions with local opinion leaders and experts. Alternatively, despite its low resolution for use
at local scale, LULC monitoring can be done regularly every 5 years. This can be modified depending on
the objective of the land use change information.
