5.3.7 Splash erosion
Raindrops that hit bare soil with full force can break apart the soil structure in a process called rain
splash erosion. As raindrops strike the ground, they break down soil particles from the larger soil mass
and scatter them in all directions. Loosened particles are then easily carried away by flowing water. This
process is the primary source of soil particles available for erosion and transport during rainfall events.


Measuring splash erosion:
X    Splash erosion can be measured in different soil types of varying erodibility. So, it is necessary to
     identify the major soil types in the watershed and locate where splash erosion measurement is to
     be conducted.
X    Characterize the soil type, vegetation (type and coverage), slope, and related topographic features,
     rainfall characteristics, etc.
X    Use a splash board, cup, or small funnels or bottles to measure rain splash erosion in the field
     (Figure 6).
X    These devices are inserted in surface soil, but care should be taken to control the contribution of
     overland flow to the splash cup and funnel when measuring splash erosion.
X    Splash erosion can also be measured by collecting detached and splashed soil materials or using
     rainfall simulators.
X    The amount of soil or sediment material collected due to splash erosion depends on the diameter of
     the measuring device. Therefore, the measured data need to be corrected considering the diameter
     of the splash cup, board, or funnel (Morgan, 2005).
