5.3.8 Inter-rill erosion
Inter-rill erosion results from uniform removal of topsoil due to overland flow. This form of erosion is
difficult to recognize in the field, but some indicators are plant root exposure, rock exposure, and change
in soil color. Inter-rill erosion is measured using micro-plots (3 m2) that represent the different soil types
(with varying erodibility) and areas receiving different rainfall amounts and inclination/rainfall direction
(Table 20). However, this measurement involving runoff plots includes the measurement of splash, sheet,
and rill erosion in combination. To measure how much soil has been lost to inter-rill erosion:
X      Use erosion pins installed in the soil, protruding above the soil and marked at the surface so that
       soil depth reduced due to inter-rill erosion can be determined.
X      Erosion pins can be installed considering spatial variation in micro-topography, soil properties, and
       land cover/land use.
X      Several replications (at least three pins) are needed for each unit of land and slope (MU).
X      Soil depth reduction is recorded and the affected area is measured to estimate the total volume of
       soil lost.
X      A 1-mm soil depth reduction corresponds to approximately 10 to 15 t/ha/year depending on soil bulk
       density.
