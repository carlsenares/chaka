Preface
Integrated watershed management (IWM) is a holistic approach to sustainably managing land, water,
and ecosystems within a watershed. It is an essential strategy for addressing challenges such as land
degradation, biodiversity loss, water scarcity, and climate change while ensuring the livelihoods and
well-being of communities. In Ethiopia, where the agricultural sector is central to the economy and rural
livelihoods, effective watershed management is critical for sustainable development.
This Integrated Watershed Management Guideline has been developed as a comprehensive resource to
standardize data collection, analysis, and management practices across the country. By establishing
uniform methodologies, this guideline aims to enhance the consistency and reliability of watershed data,
thus fostering better communication, planning, decision-making, and implementation of IWM strategies.
The creation of this guideline reflects the multi-disciplinary collaborative efforts of national and
international experts, practitioners, and development partners. Facilitated by the Ethiopian Institute
of Agricultural Research, CIAT, and GIZ, this initiative underscores the importance of partnership in
addressing complex watershed management challenges. It builds on existing frameworks and incorporates
global best practices tailored to Ethiopia's unique environmental and socioeconomic contexts.
This guideline is designed to support a wide range of stakeholders: researchers, policymakers,
development practitioners, and local communities. It provides practical tools and methodologies for
designing, collecting, and using data on hydrology, soil, land use, and socioeconomic aspects within
watersheds. By doing so, it seeks to empower stakeholders to implement integrated nature-based
solutions that balance ecological integrity with human needs.
We firmly believe that this guideline will play a pivotal role in advancing Ethiopia’s efforts to achieve
sustainable watershed management. It aligns with national priorities for environmental conservation,
climate resilience, and agricultural productivity, contributing to the broader goals of food security and
rural development nationally while addressing the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goals.
On behalf of the EIAR and all contributing partners, we extend our deepest appreciation to everyone
who contributed to this significant work. Your expertise and dedication have shaped a resource that
will serve as a foundation for integrated watershed management in Ethiopia for years to come.
Finally, we invite all stakeholders to embrace and use this guideline as a valuable tool for fostering
sustainable and inclusive development across the nation’s watersheds.

1. Introduction                                                                                       © CIAT / Neil Palmer




With a population surpassing 100 million, Ethiopia is a country whose economic growth heavily depends
on agriculture (CSA, 2016). The country has diverse topographical, geological, geomorphological, and
hydrological conditions – creating diverse agroecological zones where the central, southeastern,
and northern highlands are hilly/mountainous draining to lowlands. Because of the rapid increase
in population, most of the agricultural land is occupied and limited land is left for agricultural
production in the highlands, except through integrated hillside/mountain development. Smallholdings
for agriculture are highly fragmented and degraded, leading to low agricultural productivity and
associated water scarcity and food insecurity. The natural ecosystems (e.g., forest, shrub, grazing
land/rangeland) are alarmingly converted to agricultural production even on marginal hillslopes.
As a result, our perennial streams, reservoirs, and drinking water supplies are being degraded and
threatened.
Integrated watershed management (IWM) is a key approach to sustaining agricultural economic
development and maintaining natural resources. We need to protect and restore environmental
quality in our agricultural production systems, river basins, and other land uses. The government
recognizes that the complex interactions of natural and human systems (continuous crop cultivation,
deforestation, steep slope cultivation, unregulated water withdrawals, erosion and sedimentation,
competition for land uses, non-point source pollution, etc.) coupled with unregulated/unplanned
expansion of human settlements into fragile ecosystems have been creating new management
problems that cannot be addressed by business-as-usual practices. Human modifications of land
and water directly affect the delivery of water, sediments, and nutrients, and thus fundamentally
alter terrestrial and aquatic ecosystems. Such unregulated and unplanned management is also
contributing to hazards and disasters such as mass movements, flooding, extreme drought, and
associated problems. Addressing all the problems that concern land, water, vegetation, and other
ecosystem resources of a watershed in any way comes under watershed management strategies.
The government therefore developed the Ethiopian Strategic Investment Framework (ESIF) for
sustainable land management (SLM) and determined that a comprehensive watershed management
process would be the most efficient means to address these problems (MoARD, 2010).
Watershed management is an iterative process of integrated decision-making regarding uses and
modifications of land and water within a watershed (Joseph, 2009). This process provides a chance for
stakeholders to balance diverse goals and uses for environmental resources, and to consider how their
cumulative actions might affect the long-term sustainability of these resources. This process requires
research supported by standardized scientific methods/tools that generate evidence on the impacts
of watershed interventions. Data should be collected at different scales and documented with standard
methods and procedures. This would enable problem-solving research, promote evidence-based decisions,
address common watershed-related constraints, and support better communication among researchers.

Research data can include characterization of watersheds as well as monitoring and evaluation of
interventions. Although Ethiopia has been implementing extensive watershed management for the last
five decades, standardized data collection methods and evidence documentation are lacking. The data

are not adequate for making evidence-based decisions. This calls for reviewing and standardizing data
generation processes for IWM research and development endeavors. Thus, this standard for data collection
encompasses data collected in plot, farm, sub-watershed, and watershed studies.

2. Purpose of the Guideline
Integrated watershed management is an approach to nurture the integrated use and management of land,
vegetation, water, and other watershed resources to benefit people, protect the watershed ecosystem,
and reduce or avoid negative downstream impacts (German et al., 2007). It is meant to manage human
activities and natural resources together in a watershed as a system. Changes in environmental costs and
socioeconomic impacts result from natural soil erosion, changes in farming systems, over-abstraction of
water, overgrazing, deforestation, and pollution. Watershed management encompasses interventions that
meet diverse stakeholder and ecosystem needs under various land uses and farming systems: highland
agriculture farming system, lowland agriculture farming system, forest system, agro-pastoralist and
pastoralist system, and upstream and downstream communities. Depending on the prevailing conditions,
watershed management could have one or more objectives: agricultural production and food security,
water management and security, drought management, flood management, conservation of biodiversity,
and so on. Nevertheless, because of the strong interconnection among watershed resources, watershed
management interventions focusing on a single component of the watershed or single objective might
result in multiple and far-reaching impacts.
We recognize that watersheds constitute both human and natural systems. However, this guideline
focuses mainly on natural systems and processes (soil, water, vegetation, climate, and topography
interaction) and is limited to current socioeconomic and livelihood-related interventions. This guideline
adopts a micro-watershed scale of a minimum size of 500 ha, which is aligned with the national
community-based integrated watershed management guideline.
We used the following assumptions in preparing this guideline:
X   Many watersheds do not constitute a full-fledged data monitoring facility and have field experiments
    to employ advanced data collection methods.
X   Selected methods and procedures presented in this guideline are identified based on the technical
    capability of most experts and average watershed conditions.
X   The type of data requirements in a watershed are considered based on systems thinking.
X   Interaction of local knowledge and empirical testing methods is taken into consideration.
X   Future updates are necessary to reflect advanced methods and tools for watershed monitoring and
    evaluation (such as automatic data loggers, radar, photogrammetry, remote sensing, etc.) as well
    as the dynamics in watershed management. Regular updates ensure the workability of the data
    collection standards in relation to inclusiveness and utility by most experts and researchers.
The main purpose of standardized data collection methods for integrated watershed management is
to establish minimum common standard procedures and methods of data collection (sampling, mapping,
describing, and measuring) for a range of research, impact evaluation, and monitoring work commonly

3. Important Considerations
This standardization of data on integrated watershed management is guided by the concept and principles
of watershed management. The identification of data types and associated methods follows the principles
of watershed processes and interactions as well as accounting for the data/information demand from
diverse stakeholder perspectives and needs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the data requirement and related
methods are developed by taking into consideration the interactions within a watershed, which include
i.    Processes and interactions: topographic, hydrologic (surface and subsurface processes),
      geomorphologic, geologic, soils, climatic variables, land use and land cover, anthropogenic factors,
      and upstream-downstream issues.
ii.   Farming systems: farming system components and their spatial and temporal interactions.
iii. Scales: spatial scale (plot to watershed), temporal scale (event, daily, monthly, seasonal, and annual),
     and social scale (household, community, and upstream/downstream).
iv. Thematic and multidisciplinary aspects: geology, hydrology, geomorphology, soil, water, forest,
    and crop.
v.    Processes and interactions of natural and human factors.
vi. Major watershed-related studies and monitoring: watershed delineation, characterization (baseline),
    monitoring, evaluation, and impact.
The following elements have been considered in developing this guideline for data standardization to be
generated/collected through IWM projects and programs:
X     Scientific requirements (scientific, technical, and practical know-how): Watershed management
      solutions demand harmonized and synergistic interventions to respond to complex processes and
      interactions within watersheds. Watershed data collection should be based on sufficient technical
      and systemic knowledge and sound data management skills. It requires a strong scientific base of
      interdisciplinary, transdisciplinary, analytical, and systems knowledge, which are produced with the
      integration of local knowledge.
X     Base maps: Topographic base maps of watersheds/micro-watersheds with appropriate scale, which
      is adequate for a detailed survey within a watershed (area from 500 to 1,000 ha), are essential as they
      are the basis for benchmarking and the sampling, monitoring, and surveying of various data types
      and variables (land use, crop production, performance of soil conservation, assessment of erosion
      damage, geology, soil, etc.). To conduct different research and monitoring at different spatial units,
      precise topographic base maps at least at a scale of 1:5,000 are appropriate.

5. Standards of Data Acquisition
This section describes the standards of data acquisition: (a) unit of data sampling (data collection
unit or scale), (b) data acquisition methods and procedures (for both primary and derived parameters),
(c) unit of measurement of variables, (d) formats to be used for data collection, and (e) data quality control
procedures. Data collection templates were developed to use uniform formats and simplify database
development. Readme files and metadata were developed to provide a clear catalogue of the data.



5.1 Types of data requirements in watersheds
The types of data needed for delineating, characterizing, planning, monitoring, and evaluating watershed
management interventions depend on watershed problems and their interactions, the size and heterogeneity
of the watersheds, and management objectives. The data requirements constitute general data and thematic
physical and socioeconomic data. The following data types describe broad aspects of required data. The detailed
variables and parameters are presented in the subsequent sections under the different types of studies.
General data: Data on a watershed should include watershed name, location, administrative districts,
boundaries, size/area, elevation, presence of streams, tributaries, agroecologies, farming systems,
population, accessibility, roads, etc. The watershed might need to be divided into many sub-watersheds
depending on watershed heterogeneity and each should be assigned a number for easy identification.
Further data will be needed on a sub-watershed basis.
Basic physical data: Data on geology, soils, hydrology, and infrastructure can usually be obtained from
existing reports or assessed using a field survey. Geomorphological data such as drainage patterns,
stream density and order, channel profiles, landforms, slopes, aspect, etc., can be obtained and/or
quantified by using a good topographic map to determine hydrological conditions and land capabilities
of a watershed and appropriate conservation needs. Data on soil hydrologic conditions are required in
different land use classes such as forest, grassland, rangeland, and cultivated land.
Constraints and community perception data: In watersheds, the interaction of human and natural
systems is often complex. The community has diverse interests and needs, which involve conflicts of
interest. This complexity should be characterized in terms of prioritized constraints through community
participatory approaches. Community perception on watershed management interventions and overall
impacts is needed to meet community needs. Upstream and downstream relations need to be understood
to build benefit-sharing mechanisms.
Land degradation data: Data on the forms, distribution, and extent of land degradation are essential to
characterize the severity of land uses and prioritize areas of the watershed for management interventions.
These data are also useful to determine relationships with trends of production, ecosystem services,
and food insecurity.

Climate, hydrology, and water resources data: Climatic data such as precipitation, wind, evaporation,
temperature, humidity, etc., can normally be obtained from climatic stations in the watershed or nearby
stations. For watershed management, especially for runoff estimation and design of erosion control
measures, rainfall data (such as direction, amount, and intensity) are required. In this case, some automatic
rain gauges might need to be placed in the watershed area distributed based on rainfall variations and rainfall
directions in the whole watershed. Soil moisture dynamics and infiltration characteristics of watersheds
are needed to understand soil and climate relations and the resulting hydrology of the watershed. Data
on surface runoff, stream flows, and sedimentation are often not available in upstream watersheds and
there is a need to collect data from fields and stations at sub-catchments and outlets of streams. Data
on hydrogeology and groundwater levels should be evaluated and are often associated with variations
in rock types (geological formation), geomorphology, and topographic conditions. The water balance or
water budget of the watershed should be estimated. Water use problems regarding quality or quantity
also need to be determined. For information on flood damage, landslides, drought, and other hydrologic
problems, the usual techniques are field documentation of damaged areas, interviewing people affected
by such hazards, and discussing the matter with knowledgeable institutions in the area.
Land use, land capability, and biological data: This category of data usually includes current land use,
land use history and future trends, land capability or suitability, and several vegetation surveys. For
current land use, a new survey is often required to identify forest and range cover, cropland, plantations,
rural settlements, infrastructure, urban areas, water bodies, etc. The needed data and survey criteria for
each major and detailed land use will depend on management objectives and input data requirements for
modeling purposes. Land use history is needed to reveal past lessons and experience and evaluate the
impacts of historical land uses on its status. These kinds of data can be obtained from reports, records,
or knowledgeable persons in the local community.
Future trends in land use are very important to planners and decision-makers. Land capability or
suitability data are usually required to show the limit, extent, and proper use of each piece of land in
the watershed. Although criteria might vary, they are essentially based on soil, climate, slope, and land
capability classification, which have been used in many hilly watersheds. Special biological, vegetation,
and multiple-use surveys are sometimes needed. They might cover forest resources, protection forest,
rangeland and grassland, riverine, and wildlife reservation areas.
Erosion data: Since erosion is a major concern in most watersheds, the collection of erosion data is a
very important part of the overall survey. The causes of erosion and its severity should first be identified.
Field-level runoff information and associated data on soil loss due to different forms of erosion (splash,
sheet, rill, gully) are required to determine the spatial and temporal processes that affect erosion in
the different parts of the watershed. Appropriate measurement and survey techniques should be used
regarding the collection of data on erosion (splash, sheet, rill, gully, tillage, stream, and wind) as well as
landslides and associated problems. Surveys of geology and landforms or geomorphology, both of which
are related to erosion, are needed.
Data on performance of land management practices: Individual technologies and practices should be
evaluated in terms of effectiveness and adoption to identify the most effective and adaptable options.
Specific practices or technologies, as well as overall implementation, should be evaluated in terms of
the impact on ecosystem services and community well-being.

5.2.2 Infrastructure (roads, settlements, and social services)

5.2.3 Geology, geomorphology, and hydrogeology
Understanding the geological, hydrogeological, and geomorphological conditions of a watershed is
important for the proper planning, design, implementation, and monitoring of watershed interventions.
Geological and hydrogeological parameters: The watershed should be characterized in terms of rock
and soil type, permeability, infiltration capacity, aquifer characteristics, and erodibility. This requires
evaluating the (a) type, thickness, distribution, and characteristics of the unconsolidated deposits/
soils; (b) type, thickness, distribution, degree of weathering, and permeability of the different rocks; (c)
distribution and characteristics (infill, aperture, continuity, spacing, etc.) of the major geological structures
(such as lithological contacts, faults, shear zones, foliations, bedding planes, joints, unconformities, and
other weak zones); (d) groundwater aquifer systems as well as the recharge-storage-discharge zones; (e)
inventory of existing groundwater and spring points, along with their yield and quality; and (f) erodibility
of the soils and rocks in the watershed.
Geomorphological parameters: The geomorphological parameters to be evaluated in the watershed are
(a) terrain characteristics and drainage patterns, (b) mass movement hazards (landslides, major gully
zones, piping/subsidence, etc.), and (c) flooding hazards.
Methods/approaches: The above-mentioned data could be gathered from geological and structural
mapping, hydrogeological mapping and characterization, and subsurface investigations (test pits and
trenches) (Table 5). The geological, hydrogeological, and geomorphological investigations shall include:

X      Geological, hydrogeological, and geomorphological mapping: this involves descriptions of the
       lithological conditions of the soils/rocks and structural mapping (from surface exposures, river
       sections, and geophysical survey and borehole logs, if available).

5.3.1 Climatic variables

Rainfall amount

Rainfall is measured as the amount of water that reaches the horizontal ground or the horizontal ground
projection plane of the Earth’s surface and is expressed as a vertical depth of water in mm.
Rain gauges are classified into recording and non-recording types. The latter include cylindrical and
ordinary rain gauges, and the observer manually measures precipitation with these types. Some recording
types such as siphon rain gauges have a built-in recorder, and the observer must physically visit the
observation site to obtain data. Other types such as tipping bucket rain gauges have a recorder attached
to them, and remote readings can be taken by setting a recorder at a site distant from the gauge itself
to enable automatic observation.
Rainfall data can be collected with a pluviometer at 1–1.5 m above the ground, and with a rainfall inclinometer
(Table 6). Using a pluviometer, rainfall is recorded on a monthly or bimonthly chart, and the instrument is
checked daily at 0900 for the smoothness of the recording, the calibration, and/or running of the clock.

Relative humidity is the ratio of the current absolute humidity to the highest possible absolute humidity.

Sunshine hours
Sunshine duration is the length of time that the ground surface is irradiated by direct solar radiation (i.e.,
sunlight reaching the Earth’s surface directly from the sun). In 2003, the World Meteorological Organization
(WMO) defined sunshine duration as the period during which direct solar irradiance exceeds a threshold
value of 120 W/m2. This value is equivalent to the level of solar irradiance shortly after sunrise or shortly
before sunset in cloud-free conditions. It is determined by comparing the sunshine duration recorded
using a Campbell–Stokes sunshine recorder with the actual direct solar irradiance (Figure 4).


Solar radiation
Everything in nature emits electromagnetic energy and solar radiation. The energy of extraterrestrial
solar radiation is distributed over a wide continuous spectrum ranging from ultraviolet to infrared rays.
In this spectrum, solar radiation in short wavelengths (0.29 to 3.00 µm) accounts for about 97% of the
total energy.

5.3.2 Severity and extent of land degradation
Mapping the extent and severity of land degradation in a watershed helps to identify hotspots and priority
areas of intervention. It is a basis for planning management options and helps ensure the rehabilitation
and management of high-risk and more vulnerable areas. The general purpose is to provide standardized
and objective data on land degradation; understand the nexus between land degradation, food security,
biodiversity, climate, and overall ecosystem services; and create the basis for informed advice on land
degradation at the local level.
Land degradation assessment is mostly done using Global Assessment of Soil Degradation (GLASOD) and
Land Degradation Assessment for Dryland Areas (LADA) tools, which are developed by FAO. However, they
are more appropriate for national and global assessments. For local land and water management planning,
detailed assessment of land degradation locally is important. Thus, qualitative information on the severity
and extent of land degradation can be collected using a homogeneous mapping unit of 25 m × 25 m (625 m2)
for more heterogeneous watershed areas and 30 m × 30 m (900 m2 or less) for relatively less heterogeneous
watersheds (Kassawmar et al., 2018). The physically homogeneous mapping units are identified based on
the homogeneity of combined features of land cover/uses, slope classes, soil types, landforms, geological
formation, and other natural and man-made features. The homogeneous mapping units can be delineated and
prepared in the office using high-resolution Google Earth images printed on A2 or A3 paper for field use. The
delineation of mapping units is further refined (merged or split) and improved through ground truthing using
GPS. Desta et al. (2019) describe the assessment of the degree/severity of land degradation in terms of soil
depth, vegetation cover, erosion indicators/features (sheet, rill, gully, landslide), compaction, acidity, fertility
status, agricultural suitability, and biotic functions. Estimates of the extent and severity of land degradation
for each physically homogeneous mapping unit can be made using a transect walk by a team of experts and
local people. For each unit, they discuss and describe the mapping unit in terms of the predefined criteria and
decide the four severity classes (low, moderate, high, and very high). Table 11 presents a detailed description

5.3.5 Surface runoff monitoring
At a watershed scale, surface runoff processes can be studied. The dominant runoff processes depend
on local conditions related to the catchment, slope gradients, vegetation cover, and antecedent soil
conditions such as moisture content and level of groundwater. Horton overland flow (surface runoff)
occurs when rainfall intensity exceeds the rate of infiltration capacity of the soil. This process dominates
watersheds in arid and semi-arid environments with higher rainfall, soils or rocks with low infiltration
capacity (e.g., clay soils, shales, etc.), and poorly vegetated landscapes.
Saturation overland flow occurs in those parts of the river basin where the soil is saturated throughout
its depth due to rapid infiltration and the groundwater table thus reaches the soil surface. In the case of
saturation excess conditions, saturated soil absorbs less water and excess water runs off the surface.
Saturation might be caused by local rainfall and infiltration, by subsurface water flow from higher grounds,
or by a rise in groundwater level through recharge.


Measuring runoff
Measuring surface runoff through runoff plots can be expensive in terms of cost and time. Therefore, to
be cost effective, the same runoff plots can be used
X    To measure runoff for design purposes (water harvesting)
X    To measure soil loss (conservation planning)
X    To evaluate the effectiveness of LMP
Runoff plots were commonly applied in many erosion and field hydrological studies. Long-term (several
years) soil erosion studies in the U.S. by Wischmeier and Smith (1978) fixed 4-m-wide and 22-m-long
plots as a standard at 9% slope. The SCRP in Ethiopia adapted 2-m-wide and 15-m-long runoff plots.
Reviewing these against the topography, cultivation and tillage practices, and rainfall-runoff relations,
we recommend runoff plot sizes of at least 4 m × 15 m for slopes <20% and 4 m × 10 m for slopes >20%.
We use 4-m width rather than 2 m to capture the effect of off-contour tillage management on runoff
concentration. Also, narrow runoff plots suffer from border effects with respect to data reliability. The
suggestion of 10-m- or 15-m-long plots is concentrated in rill formation and development starting within
10 m or sometimes 5–6 m on steep slopes and within 15 m on gentle slopes (Gessesse et al., 2015; 2010).

5.3.7 Splash erosion
Raindrops that hit bare soil with full force can break apart the soil structure in a process called rain
splash erosion. As raindrops strike the ground, they break down soil particles from the larger soil mass
and scatter them in all directions. Loosened particles are then easily carried away by flowing water. This
process is the primary source of soil particles available for erosion and transport during rainfall events.

5.3.10 Rill erosion
In this guideline, we adopted Herweg’s Assessment of Current Erosion Damage (ACED) to determine rill
erosion dimensions and rill erosion hotspots. Data on rill dimension (length, width, depth), rill density and
distribution, and rill damage area can be assessed using ACED, which has two major purposes:
1.   supplement existing erosion measurement data at different levels, such as test plots and river
     gauging stations, and
2.   provide practitioners with a more cost-effective tool to assess soil erosion and draw conclusions
     about implementing SWC measures.
Being a rough method of soil erosion assessment, ACED cannot provide the same level of accuracy as
test plots or stream/river gauging. The level of accuracy can be ±15% and accuracy further decreases up
to ±30% for less experienced researchers/experts, when vegetation cover percentage increases or when
the rills become more complex. ACED or hotspot damage mapping involves point-linear measurement,
measuring soil loss at critical locations during severe rainstorms. It is a short-term monitoring method
and results often indicate hotspot erosion areas.

5.3.12 Tillage erosion
The effects of water and wind erosion are often dramatic and can easily be identified in the field.
However, the effects and severity of tillage erosion become apparent only after several decades of tillage
operation and are less observable by farmers in the field. During tillage operation, soils are redistributed
by tillage of various implements used in both mechanized and non-mechanized agriculture. The rates
of soil translocation by tillage depend on tillage depth, type of tillage implement, slope, speed of tillage
operation, direction and frequency of tillage, and soil properties. Tillage erosion can be of the same
magnitude as water erosion. Tillage erosion operates like a conveyor belt, transferring soil and associated
constituents from convexity to concavity (Figure 7). Tillage erosion is expressed in terms of tillage
transport coefficient (Qs), which is mostly constant and ranges on the order of 400–800 kg/m/year for
mechanized farm implements and 70–260 kg/m/year for a non-mechanized implement.

5.3.15 Wind erosion
Wind erosion is a process in which powerful air currents lift soil particles from the ground and carry
them to new locations. Transportation by wind can be in the form of suspension, saltation, or surface
creep depending on particle size. Large particles greater than 500 µm are transported by surface creep,
rolling in contact with the ground surface, whereas intermediate particles (50–100 µm) are transported
by saltation or series jump, and small particles in suspension.
Wind erosion occurs when the soil is dry, is low in organic matter content, and has low vegetation cover
and surface roughness. In Ethiopia, although water erosion processes such as sheet, rill, and gully
are important in the central and north highlands, wind erosion is important in the peripheral lowlands
of the country, mainly in the Rift Valley and rift escarpment. Erosion science for wind erosion and its
measurement methods are limited in Ethiopia.

flow downslope. To measure the amount of sediment accumulated/retained by the structures, two
independent methods can be used:
1.   For structures with runoff drainage ditches on the upper side (e.g., graded soil bunds and stone bunds).
2.   For those without runoff ditches (e.g., fanya juu, grass strip, bench terrace).

amount is a fraction of the soil loss washed from the terraced area and part of the total soil loss
washed down and transported out along the gradient. The volume of sediment accumulated per unit
length of structure can then be estimated by multiplying the depth of the sediment by the cross-
sectional area of the ditch.

could be measured by either of the following two methods:
1.   Before the start of rain, lay out a 1-m plastic sheet at three points (center of 10-m intervals) along a
     30-m-long sediment accumulation area. Measure the volume of sediment using buckets or weight
     of sediment every 2 weeks until the end of the rainy season.
2.   Before the start of rain, install enough graduated/marked wooden pegs or iron pins at 10-m
     intervals along the 30-m length (with a minimum of three pegs per interval). Measure the
     depth of accumulated sediment every 2 weeks until the end of the rainy season. The volume of
     sediment retained is estimated by multiplying the average depth by the sediment accumulation
     area (Tables 28 and 29).

5.4.6 Tree parameters and habitat
Watershed management primarily focuses on maintaining natural vegetation and restoring degraded
lands through vegetation cover. As such, tree plantation is a key restoration strategy implemented
through enrichment plantations, enclosures, woodlots, and agroforestry.
Key tree parameters for monitoring include survival rate, growth rate, regeneration, species composition,
species diversity, species richness, and species abundance. These tree data are collected at different locations:
(a) on degraded lands to assess natural regeneration rates, (b) on plantation areas to evaluate growth rate, and
(c) on rehabilitated and vegetated areas to analyze composition, diversity, and distribution of tree species.
Sampling: A systematic sampling design, with random starting points, can be used to collect vegetation
data. Sampling can be done in a line transect at regular intervals. Along each line transect, landscape
positions (upper slope, mid-slope, and foot slope) can be selected. The number of transects and transect



 66

intervals depends on the objective and heterogeneity of vegetation patterns at the site. Transects are
laid out parallel to the topography. At each landscape position, quadrats measuring 20 m × 20 m are used
and placed at certain intervals (to avoid edge effects of other land uses).


Growth parameters
In each of the quadrats (20 m × 20 m), individual plant variables such as count/survival rate per season,
diameter at breast height (DBH) for smaller and multi-stemmed shrubs, diameter at stump height or at
a height of 30 cm (d 30) from the ground, crown diameter, and total height of all woody species can be
measured using diameter tape and clinometers, respectively.


Richness and diversity
The identity and abundance of seedlings and saplings can be identiﬁed in the ﬁeld using the published
volumes of the Flora of Ethiopia as well as Flora of Ethiopia and Eritrea and counted from subplots
with 5 m × 5 m (at least two subplots) in the main quadrats. For those diffcult to identify in the ﬁeld,
specimens are collected, processed, and identiﬁed by comparing them with already identiﬁed specimens
deposited in the National Herbarium of Ethiopia. The types of plant species found at the study sites
are also described in terms of richness, plant family, and habitat. The cover of herbaceous species is
estimated in five subplots of 1 m × 1 m in the four corners and center of the main quadrats of 20 m × 20
m. To determine light availability, canopy openness can be measured using a densitometer positioned
in the center of each quadrant.
The average total density of plant species per hectare is derived from the total number of individuals
recorded in the sample plots. Sorensen’s similarity index (Sorensen, 1948) is used to determine
species similarities. The Shannon-Wiener diversity index (H0; Shannon, 1948) is commonly used to
determine native plant species diversity and to conduct comparisons in species diversity among
the studied sites. To determine similarity in abundance of different species within a community, we
can use the Shannon-Wiener index of evenness (J). The regeneration capacity of restored sites for
the trees and shrubs can be detected from soil seed bank samples and the regeneration inventory
of species in the field.


Aboveground biomass and carbon estimation
To estimate aboveground biomass, inventory data are used to identify dominant woody species. Then, the
methods of Hoff et al. (2002) and Snowdon et al. (2002) for aboveground woody biomass measurement
are used. We advise grouping the dominant woody species into diameter classes to minimize errors
that can arise from variable sizes of individual tree/shrub species. From the different land use sites,
selected individual species representing dominant species can be harvested and weighed. Fresh mass of
aboveground vegetation is then adjusted to dry mass using the measured moisture content, determined
by oven-drying samples at 65 oC until constant mass is attained. Carbon stocks in aboveground biomass
are estimated by multiplying the oven-dried biomass by a factor of 0.5 (Snowdon et al., 2002).

6. Advances in Standardized Data Collection
   for Integrated Watershed Management
6.1 Introduction
Integrated watershed management is essential in sustainably managing water, soil, and other natural
resources. Although the effectiveness of IWM depends heavily on data quality and consistency, developing
countries have historically struggled with limited resources, outdated methods, and inconsistent
measurement methods. The emergence of cost-effective technologies and standardized practices,
however, is transforming how data are collected, shared, and used.
This section explores how technological advances (e.g., low-cost devices – free satellite data, mobile
tools, and open-access platforms – along with improved data standardization practices) can be mobilized
in developing countries to create a strong foundation for sustainable watershed management.



6.2 Affordable IoT solutions for soil and water monitoring
The landscape of hydrological data collection has evolved significantly. Where expensive equipment once
created barriers for many regions, affordable Internet of Things devices have now democratized access to
real-time environmental monitoring. These devices, including low-cost weather stations, portable water
quality sensors, and soil moisture probes, provide data on key parameters such as rainfall, streamflow,
groundwater level, and soil moisture content.
OpenWeatherMap exemplifies this shift by providing affordable weather data access while community
initiatives such as the EnviroDIY Mayfly Data Logger enable local monitoring at significantly reduced costs
(Musah et al., 2022). IoT networks facilitate remote data access, thus decreasing the financial burden
of frequent field visits to remote or rural areas. Through consistent calibration and standard reporting
protocol, these tools ensure reliable and comparable data across various regions (Hossain et al., 2021).



6.3 Using satellite imagery for watershed monitoring
Satellite imagery has become a critical watershed management tool. Free and open-access platforms
such as Sentinel-2 (European Space Agency) and Landsat (NASA) provide consistent and standardized
information on land use, vegetation cover, and water bodies. These tools are particularly beneficial for
developing countries with limited budgets for high-resolution spatial data. Platforms such as Google
Earth Engine enable stakeholders to analyze satellite images without requiring advanced computing
infrastructure. Indices such as NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) can track vegetation

health, while DEMs from sources such as SRTM (Shuttle Radar Topography Mission) support erosion
and watershed flow analysis (Gorelick et al., 2017). These tools ensure consistent global standards while
empowering local stakeholders to implement evidence-based watershed management strategies (Roy
et al., 2016).



6.4 Mobile tools for community-driven data collection
In developing countries, local community participation in data collection is critical for success. Mobile-
based tools such as KoboToolbox and Open Data Kit (ODK) allow non-experts to collect environmental
and socioeconomic data in standardized formats. These apps can capture GPS locations, photos, and
other metadata automatically, thus ensuring consistency across datasets.
Community groups can use these mobile applications to record rainfall patterns, monitor soil conditions,
or log observations on water quality. These tools are easy to use, even in areas with limited technical
capacity, and their data can be integrated into larger regional or national databases for analysis (Tabbush,
2018). This approach not only standardizes data but also fosters local ownership and participation in
watershed management (Tabbush, 2018).



6.5 Hydrological models
Standardized hydrological watershed models such as SWAT (Soil and Water Assessment Tool) and
WEPP (Water Erosion Prediction Project) have made it easier to simulate and predict the impact of
various land and water management interventions. These models require standardized inputs, such as
soil characteristics, land use, land cover, DEM, and climate information, to ensure globally consistent
and comparable outputs.
In sub-Saharan Africa, SWAT has been used successfully to predict the impact of agricultural practices
on water quality and soil erosion. These models enable decision-makers to test different scenarios, such
as reforestation or irrigation changes, before implementing them in the field (Flanagan et al., 2007).



6.6 Open data platforms
Global databases and open-access platforms provide ready-made datasets for regions with limited
capacity for primary data collection. FAO’s AQUASTAT provides standardized water statistics, while
HydroSHEDS offers hydrological datasets such as river networks and watershed boundaries. These
platforms simplify access to high-quality datasets while diminishing dependency on expensive local
data collection efforts (Lehner et al., 2008).
Such platforms prove especially valuable in areas with underdeveloped local monitoring networks.
By integrating these global datasets with local observations, watershed planners can create more
comprehensive and reliable management plans (FAO, 2022).



6.7 Climate data integration
Climate change’s impact on watersheds makes the integration of standardized climate data essential.
Free resources such as CHIRPS (Climate Hazards Group InfraRed Precipitation with Station Data) and
CMIP6 (Coupled Model Intercomparison Project) provide reliable historical and future climate projections.
These datasets are invaluable for forecasting risks such as floods, droughts, and changes in seasonal
water availability (Funk et al., 2015; Eyring et al., 2016).

thus helping decision-makers account for climate variability and build resilience into their strategies.



6.8 Citizen science programs
Citizen science initiatives, in which volunteers can collect data from local landscapes, help not only in
gathering data but also in raising community awareness on watershed management issues. This approach
can provide real-time information about a given watershed using various tools such as mobile apps.



6.9 Artificial intelligence and machine learning
Artificial intelligence and machine learning are revolutionizing watershed data collection through several
innovative applications. By leveraging AI and ML in various methods, watershed researchers and managers
can gather more accurate data. The following are some of the applications of AI and ML:
X    AI algorithms can analyze historical water quality data to predict future conditions. For instance,
     researchers in the Chesapeake Bay watershed use ML models to forecast nutrient runoff based
     on weather patterns, land use, and agricultural practices. This helps in proactive management of
     water quality.
X    AI can optimize the deployment and management of IoT sensors placed in watersheds. These
     sensors collect real-time data on streamflow, soil moisture content, and rainfall. In regions such
     as the Mississippi River Basin, ML algorithms can analyze these data to detect anomalies, such as
     unexpected spikes in turbidity, indicating pollution events.
X    AI-driven image recognition tools can analyze drone or camera trap footage to monitor wildlife and
     habitats within watersheds. In the Sierra Nevada mountains, ML algorithms help track the presence
     and movement of species that are sensitive to changes in water availability and quality.
X    AI chatbots and mobile applications can facilitate community reporting of pollution or habitat
     degradation. These data can then be aggregated and analyzed using ML to identify hotspots and
     trends across a watershed, thus empowering local stakeholders in environmental stewardship.
X    Machine learning techniques can process large volumes of satellite imagery to assess land cover
     changes and monitor vegetation health. For example, NASA’s Earth Observing System Data and
     Information System uses AI to detect changes in watershed surfaces, thus enabling rapid identification
     of deforestation or urbanization impacts.
X    Machine learning can enhance flood forecasting by analyzing historical rainfall and river flow data
     alongside real-time meteorological data. For instance, the National Oceanic and Atmospheric
     Administration (NOAA) employs AI models to predict flooding in watersheds, thus allowing timely
     alerts and resource allocation.

7. Summary and Ways Forward
7.1     Analysis of biophysical problems
Brief and essential interpretations are needed for the data collected. The data then need to be analyzed
and interpreted in relation to relevant watershed problems or management objectives. Data should be
analyzed in relation to the most important biophysical problems in a watershed as described as follows.
Land use capability: Determining proper land use based on land capability or suitability is always the
first step toward the protection and development of a watershed. A land use adjustment map can be
produced by superimposing the current land use against land capability maps. This can help identify
areas of serious overuse requiring urgent intervention to reverse degradation. It can pinpoint underused
land with potential for increased economic output. The map provides the basis for appropriate use of
watershed lands and those that require soil conservation measures.
Water resources and hydrology analysis: From the basic data collected, stream flow patterns can be
analyzed, including annual/seasonal variations, maximum and minimum discharge rates, and water
quality indicators such as turbidity and pollutants. Flood and drought patterns should be studied. Water
use concerns, including consumption rates and quality, must be addressed. Findings can be compiled
in an annual water resources and hydrology reports.
Hazards of erosion and sedimentation: The various sources and damages of erosion and sedimentation
should be identified and analyzed, and potential hazards identified for successful conservation planning.
The latter is very important since most watershed rehabilitation or protection is centered on the
minimization of potential hazards. Special efforts should be made to analyze data collected on erosion
and sedimentation. Data can be collected from field surveys, observations, interviews, soil loss and
runoff plot data analysis, erosion modeling and prediction, storm frequency analysis, sediment delivery
assessment, reservoir sedimentation data, and geological analysis for landslide risk. Treatment costs
and potential benefits of control measures should be evaluated.



7.2 Maps, statistics, and interpretations
Maps and statistics are a major product of watershed data and watershed biophysical surveys. The kinds
of maps and statistics produced depend on watersheds and management objectives. Key documentation
should include maps showing baseline information (boundaries, sub-watersheds, villages, roads, etc.);
topography (contours, elevations, landforms, streams, etc.); soil characteristics (types, depths, fertility,
etc.); climate data (rainfall, temperature, evapotranspiration, etc.); a geology map (rock types, structures,
displacement, morphology, etc.); slope classifications; current land use and coverage; land suitability
and capability; land use adjustment recommendations; erosion and sediment source locations; hydro-
