5.3.4 Mapping land management practices
Research on land management practices (LMP) requires a multi-level monitoring approach, including
intra-terrace, plot, field, landscape positions, sub-watershed, and watershed scales. Results gained with
these multi-level monitoring methods eventually need to be interpreted together to get an overall idea
of the order of performance, distribution, and magnitude of soil loss reduction and sediment retained
by individual LMP and integrated ones under different conditions.


Type and distribution of land management practices and use rights
Data on the coverage of LMP and effectiveness are found to be relevant for evaluating the appropriateness
of practices and performance of watersheds after intervention (Table 15). Detailed homogeneous MUs of
500 m2 (up to 1,000 m2 for less heterogeneous watersheds) can be used to identify the type and distribution
of LMP that are implemented in watersheds (Table 16). Each MU is defined based on the homogeneity
of the landscape’s basic characteristics such as slope, land use/cover, soil erosion features, landforms,
and soil types. These factors are assumed to influence the placement and selection of LMP. The MUs are
developed by applying screen digitization on high-resolution Google Earth images and then produced
on A2 or A3 paper for fieldwork. During field surveys using GPS, attributes of LMP (type, count, extent
of coverage of practices, quality), land use rights (use right, land fragmentation/number of parcels per
MU), and other attributes can be directly collected based on expert judgment and local knowledge for
each MU through a transect walk. To facilitate data collection on LMP, guiding checklists are used. This
whole process shall involve consultation with local experts and the community.
