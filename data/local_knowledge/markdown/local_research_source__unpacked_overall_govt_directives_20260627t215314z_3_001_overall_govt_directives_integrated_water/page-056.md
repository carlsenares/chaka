5.3.17 Infiltration
Infiltration is the most difficult parameter to determine accurately and needs considerable time for
determination (Table 26). In determining infiltration capacity of soils, it is necessary to consider variation
in soil infiltration rates as well as groundwater conditions along landscapes. Common techniques for
measuring infiltration are (a) double ring infiltrometer, (b) mini disk infiltrometer, (c) (inverted) auger hole
methods, and (d) micro-plot methods. Other methods include ponding, blocked recirculating infiltrometers,
and the tailwater hydrograph. (For detailed information, refer to Walker, 1989)

5.4.1 Evaluating quality and efficiency of SWC practices at watershed scale
Purpose: Quality assessments evaluate how well soil and water conservation (SWC) meet design standards
and conservation goals. This monitoring provides data on their effectiveness in reducing soil loss,
managing watershed sediment, and enhancing groundwater recharge. The results identify maintenance
needs and validate the impact of conservation practices.
Sampling: To evaluate the quality and efficiency of SWC practices or technologies (mechanical and
biological) at watershed scale, divide the watershed into homogeneous sub-catchments based on factors
that affect the placement and design of SWC measures such as land use, soil drainage/texture, slope,
landform, and geology.
