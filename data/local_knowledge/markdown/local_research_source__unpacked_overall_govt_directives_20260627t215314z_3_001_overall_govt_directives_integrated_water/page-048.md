Guideline for Integrated Watershed Management Data Collection



5.3.10 Rill erosion
In this guideline, we adopted Herweg’s Assessment of Current Erosion Damage (ACED) to determine rill
erosion dimensions and rill erosion hotspots. Data on rill dimension (length, width, depth), rill density and
distribution, and rill damage area can be assessed using ACED, which has two major purposes:
1.   supplement existing erosion measurement data at different levels, such as test plots and river
     gauging stations, and
2.   provide practitioners with a more cost-effective tool to assess soil erosion and draw conclusions
     about implementing SWC measures.
Being a rough method of soil erosion assessment, ACED cannot provide the same level of accuracy as
test plots or stream/river gauging. The level of accuracy can be ±15% and accuracy further decreases up
to ±30% for less experienced researchers/experts, when vegetation cover percentage increases or when
the rills become more complex. ACED or hotspot damage mapping involves point-linear measurement,
measuring soil loss at critical locations during severe rainstorms. It is a short-term monitoring method
and results often indicate hotspot erosion areas.


Procedures:
X    Conduct a quick transect survey over the watershed.
X    Identify areas with active rill erosion damage, concentrated flow, and other vulnerable areas, and
     geo-reference these locations.
X    Divide the rills into uniform segments based on the cross-sectional area and depth of the rill.
X    For each segment, measure the depth, width, and length and calculate the volume of the rill for each
     segment.
X    Sum up the volume of all the segments and calculate the total volume of the rill.
X    Since soil loss is reported in units of mass per unit area and time, the volume should be multiplied
     by representative soil bulk density to convert from the volume unit to the mass unit.



5.3.11 Gully and bank gully survey
In gully damage areas, source area, head cut and gully-head retreat, gully network/pattern, and segments
of gully dimensions (length, width, depth) are parameters to be measured (Table 23).


Procedures:
X    Divide the main gully into uniform segments at every 10-m interval depending on the uniformity of
     the gully in its width and depth.
X    For each gully and bank gully segment, measure the width, depth, and length and calculate the
     volume (m3).
X    Sum up all the calculated volumes to obtain the total volume (m3).
X    Multiply the total volume by a representative soil bulk density to calculate soil loss in the mass unit.
X    Since gullies may not be created due to annual rainfall, proper documentation of the age of the gully
     is important through discussions/interviews with the local community. This will help to establish
     long-term soil loss rate and factors triggering gullies in the watershed.
