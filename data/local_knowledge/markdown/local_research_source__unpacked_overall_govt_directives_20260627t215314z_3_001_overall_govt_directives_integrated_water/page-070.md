Farmers’ participatory watershed assessment involves the use of farmers’ knowledge base and farmer-
expert joint learning for technology evaluation and improvement, adoption, and extension services. The
procedure is designed to integrate the knowledge, attitudes, and preferences of farmers.
The farmer participatory approach is useful to
X    Provide an opportunity for integrating technical solutions with farmers’ preferences and local
     knowledge.
X    Facilitate farmer-participatory planning and implementation.
X    Facilitate the acceptance, integration into the farming system, transfer, and application of technical
     findings and recommendations.
X    Persuade farmers to adapt technologies without external enforcement.
X    Share lessons and innovations from innovative farmers through popularization.
X    Enable empowerment of the community in environmental knowledge and enhance bottom-up
     communication.
X    Minimize the burden of development agents as farmers play the role of extension agents.
X    Help to minimize farmers’ sense of dependency in conserving their land.

The approach involves the following methodological procedures:
X    Self-confidence-building measures: This is related to awareness and attitude change activities
     to motivate farmers to engage in genuine participation and build trust. Self-confidence-building
     measures are carried out in the form of questions and answers.
X    Facilitation role of researchers and extension agents: Extension agents and researchers play a
     role in facilitating motivation and exchange of knowledge and principles. They avoid a top-down
     imposition of technical knowledge. Moreover, kebele administration bodies help to facilitate and
     organize farmers’ interests.
X    Team/group formation and participation of land users: Participation is such that all farmers are
     involved in the key collective decision-making processes. In addition, if it is difficult and unmanageable
     to involve all farmers, selected farmers’ teams can be organized to participate actively and engage
     in decision-making throughout the processes. In both cases, farmers are active leaders of the
     participation process. Watershed user cooperatives should be established to facilitate the tasks.
     Empowering cooperatives will lay the foundation for the exit strategy of any project or program.
X    Practically oriented knowledge sharing and upgrading: Farmers are involved in exploring their
     practical experience and knowledge about watershed-level processes and interactions, derivers,
     and impacts through periodical meetings, field visits, and subsequent discussions; monitoring and
     measuring necessary processes; and evaluating interventions. All land users should be involved in
     one-to-one and group visits, discussions, and implementation activities on their own farms and
     adjacent farmlands.
X    Integrating assessment of field and toposequence units: In smallholder farming systems, decision-
     making begins on the individual farm with the individual farmer as the starting point for planning.
